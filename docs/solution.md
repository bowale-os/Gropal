Problem Statement
Young adults aged 18–25 are entering financial independence for the first time. They have income, ambition, and access to more financial content than any previous generation — but no clear system to turn any of it into action.
The result is a generation that isn't financially ignorant. They're financially stuck.

They compare themselves to peers based on visible spending — apartments, cars, vacations — and assume they're behind. The Emotional Reality
Young adults constantly ask themselves:
"Can I afford this?"
"Am I behind everyone else?"
"Why is my account always low?"
"What should I actually be doing right now?"
"How do I move out, pay off debt, or start investing — safely?"
They don't avoid financial planning because they don't care. They avoid it because it feels confusing, risky, distant, and not designed for their reality — irregular income, gig work, no safety net, and no one showing them the path.
What This Costs Them
Every month of delay quietly compounds against them. Uninformed credit decisions take years to repair. Skipped employer matches and early investing windows don't come back. Protection gaps turn manageable setbacks into financial crises — a fender bender becomes a $5,000 debt, an ER visit becomes a goal-ending catastrophe. Young adults overestimate insurance costs by as much as 10–12 times the actual price, so they never even explore coverage they could easily afford. And the psychological cost — the ambient anxiety of never feeling in control of money — shapes how an entire generation relates to their own future.
The Gap
The tools that exist today all share the same fatal flaw: they show up after the money is already gone.
Budgeting apps summarize last month. Banks show a balance. Investment platforms assume you have money to invest. Financial literacy apps teach concepts disconnected from real decisions. None of them are present at the three moments that actually determine a young adult's financial trajectory:
The moment they're about to pay — when a single notification with context could change the decision
The moment they have a question — when they need a personalized answer, not a Google search
The moment their life changes — when their goals and risks need to recalculate, not stay frozen in a static budget
Young adults don't need another app that watches them spend and reports back. They need a system that's in the room when the decision happens — one that knows their goals, understands their risks, speaks their language, and gives them the power to make every dollar intentional.
That's the gap fortifi fills.

Core Features
1. Payment Interception Layer — "The Tap Check"
fortifi integrates with your payment system (Apple Pay, Google Pay, linked debit/credit cards). When you're about to make a purchase, the app evaluates the transaction against your financial state in real time and sends a contextual notification before you confirm.
This isn't a generic spending alert. It's a decision layer built on everything the app knows about you — your goals, your risks, your limits, your current cash flow position.
What this looks like:
You tap your phone to pay $120 at a store. Notification: "This puts you $45 over your weekly discretionary limit. Your Move-Out Goal shifts back 5 days. Proceed / Pause / Adjust?"
You tap to pay $30 for food delivery. Notification: "Within budget. No goal impact. ✓"
You tap to pay $600 for a flight. Notification: "This exceeds your self-set spending cap. You asked fortifi to flag anything over $200. Want to proceed with override, pause 24 hours, or split across two pay periods?"
Why this wins: No competitor operates at the point of sale. Budgeting apps tell you what you spent last week. fortifi tells you what you're about to do right now and what it costs your future. The app is present at the only moment where behavior actually changes — the moment before money leaves.

2. Conversational Financial AI — "Ask fortifi"
Users interact with their financial data through natural conversation — typed or voice — like talking to an advisor who knows everything about their money.
The AI layer sits on top of the user's complete financial profile: income, expenses, debts, goals, risk levels, spending history, and stage progression. It answers in context, not in generic advice.
What this looks like:
"Hey fortifi, can I afford a $6,000 car?" "Based on your current savings rate and monthly obligations, a $6,000 car would require either 4 months of dedicated saving or a $280/month payment that would push your emergency fund goal back by 3 months. Want me to simulate both options?"
"Should I pay off my credit card or add to savings this month?" "Your credit utilization is at 68%, which is your top risk right now. Paying $200 toward your card drops it to 52% and earns you 35 XP. Your savings goal only shifts by 4 days. I'd recommend the card this month."
"How am I doing?" "You've made 18 moves this month, 14 pointed at your goals. You're 12 days closer to your Move-Out Goal than you were on the 1st. Your biggest risk dropped from high to moderate. You're 80 XP from reaching Stability Architect."
Why this wins: Young adults don't want to interpret dashboards. They want to ask a question and get a straight answer based on their actual situation. This is the financial advisor experience that previously required $5,000 minimums and a quarterly meeting — delivered instantly, personally, and in plain language.

3. Dynamic Goal Routing — "Financial GPS"
Users set goals during onboarding and can add, update, or remove goals at any time. fortifi builds an optimized route to each goal — a step-by-step roadmap showing what to do, in what order, and by when — then recalculates the route whenever something changes.
New job? Route updates. Unexpected expense? Route adjusts. Added a new goal? The system rebalances priorities across all active goals and shows the tradeoffs.
What this looks like:
Active Goals:
Move out by December → 62% complete, on track
Pay off credit card → 40% complete, 2 weeks behind (flagged)
Start investing $50/month → Unlocks at Stability Architect stage
Suggested next move: Redirect $40 from discretionary to credit card this week. This gets Goal 2 back on schedule without affecting Goal 1's timeline.
Why this wins: Existing goal-tracking apps let you set a target and watch a progress bar. fortifi treats goals like destinations in a GPS — it doesn't just show distance remaining, it shows the optimized route, reroutes when conditions change, and tells you the next turn. Users feel guided, not just tracked.

4. Habit Engine — "Daily Builds"
Financial growth doesn't only happen when money moves. It happens when behavior changes. fortifi includes a habit system that gives users small, consistent daily actions — "builds" — that strengthen their financial position even between paychecks.
During onboarding (and as stages progress), fortifi assigns 2–3 active habits based on the user's current stage, top risk, and goals. Habits are small enough to complete in under 60 seconds but meaningful enough to compound over time.
What this looks like:
Your Active Builds: 🔄 No-Spend Check-In — Log whether today was a no-spend day (daily, 10 sec) 📊 Bill Forecast — Review upcoming bills for the next 7 days (Mon/Thu, 30 sec) 💡 One Thing I Learned — fortifi surfaces one financial concept relevant to your stage (daily, 45 sec)
Streak: 11 days → "You've checked in 11 days straight. Users who maintain a 14-day streak are 3x more likely to hit their first goal on time."
Habits rotate and evolve as the user progresses. A Credit Builder stage user might have habits around checking utilization and reviewing statements. A Stability Architect user might have habits around reviewing insurance gaps or tracking savings rate. The habits grow with you — they're never the same list for long.
Completing daily builds earns XP (small but consistent), feeds into streaks visible on the social layer, and keeps the user engaged with their financial trajectory on days when no money is moving.
Why this wins: Every financial app loses users between paychecks. There's nothing to do, so people stop opening it. Daily builds solve the "dead zone" problem — the app has a reason to exist every single day, not just on paydays. And because the habits are tied to stage and risk, they're not busywork — they're quietly rewiring financial behavior one day at a time.

5. Smart Alternatives — "Better Yes"
When a user attempts a large purchase that significantly impacts their goals, fortifi doesn't just warn them — it offers smarter paths to the same outcome. The app recognizes that the user still has a real need or want behind the purchase and helps them meet it without derailing their financial plan.
What this looks like:
User tries to buy a $22,000 car. fortifi: "This purchase pushes your emergency fund goal back by 8 months and increases your debt risk to high."
Instead of just Proceed / Pause, the user sees:
🅰️ Downgrade Option — "A $14,000 certified pre-owned vehicle meets the same need. Goal impact: 3 months instead of 8. Here are 3 listings near you."
🅱️ Delay + Save Route — "If you save $400/month for 5 months, you can put 15% down and reduce your monthly payment by $120. Your other goals stay on track."
🅲️ Restructure Route — "Shift your goal priorities: pause investing contributions for 4 months, redirect to the car fund, then resume. Net delay on investment goal: 4 months. Net delay on car: 0."
🅳️ Proceed Anyway — Full autonomy, always available.
The alternatives are generated from the user's actual financial data — income, savings rate, active goals, risk levels. They're not generic tips like "have you considered buying used?" They're specific, actionable paths with real numbers attached.
For smaller purchases, alternatives scale down:
User tries to buy a $300 jacket. "This puts you $80 over your self-set monthly clothing limit."
🅰️ Wait for it — "This item may go on sale in 3 weeks based on typical retail cycles. Set a reminder?" 🅱️ Budget shift — "Move $80 from your dining budget this month. No goal impact." 🅲️ Proceed — Override with full context.
Why this wins: The current generation of financial apps treats big purchases as pass/fail — either you can afford it or you can't. Real financial decisions aren't binary. Users don't want to be told "no." They want to be shown a better path to "yes." Smart Alternatives turns fortifi from a financial gatekeeper into a financial strategist — and that's the relationship that keeps users loyal long-term.

6. Self-Set Spending Limits — "Voluntary Guardrails"
Users set their own spending caps — by category, by time period, or overall. fortifi enforces these limits through the payment interception layer, requiring a conscious override to exceed them.
This is a commitment device: the user makes a decision about future behavior while thinking clearly, and the app holds them to it when temptation arrives.
What this looks like:
User sets: "No more than $150/week on food delivery." On Thursday, they've spent $140. Next food delivery tap triggers: "You're $10 from your self-set food limit this week. You set this limit to protect your Move-Out Goal. Override / Wait until Monday?"
The override is always available — autonomy is never removed. But the friction is intentional. The user has to consciously choose to break a promise they made to themselves, which behavioral research shows reduces impulsive spending significantly.
Why this wins: No app lets users set hard personal limits that activate at the point of payment. This is the difference between a budget you check after the fact and a boundary that protects you in the moment.

7. Social Progression Groups — "Squad XP"
Users create or join groups with friends. The group feed shows achievements, stage upgrades, streaks, and XP milestones — never income, balances, or specific spending.
What this looks like:
🎯 Marcus just hit Credit Builder stage 🔥 Amaya is on a 15-day move streak 💪 Your squad earned 340 XP this week — top 20% of all groups 🏆 Challenge active: "Emergency Fund Sprint" — 3/5 members on track
Why this wins: Financial apps are solo experiences, which is why people abandon them. fortifi makes financial growth a shared, visible journey — but protected. You see effort, consistency, and milestones. You never see anyone's actual money. This creates accountability and motivation without shame or comparison anxiety. Friends pulling each other forward is the retention mechanic that no notification system can replicate.

Supporting Infrastructure (Under the Hood)
These features power the core experience but aren't the headline — they're what make the headline features intelligent:
Financial XP System — Every move that reduces risk or advances a goal earns XP. XP drives stage progression and fuels the social layer. No XP for trivial actions — only meaningful financial behavior.
Life-Stage Progression Map — Users progress through stages (Dependent Explorer → Independent Operator) based on moves and risk reduction. Each stage unlocks new capabilities in the app (investing tools, insurance insights, advanced simulations). Stages answer the question "Am I on track?" without comparing wealth.
Risk Monitoring Engine — Tracks cash flow, debt, credit, income volatility, and protection gaps in the background. Risk never appears as a dashboard — it surfaces inside the conversational AI, the payment interception, and the goal routing as context for decisions.
Stress Test Onboarding — The first two minutes of the app show the user how their finances break under pressure. This creates emotional urgency, assigns a starting stage, identifies the top risk, and motivates the first goal. The onboarding isn't a form — it's a confrontation.

Insurance Integration — Protection Woven Into the Journey
Insurance doesn't get its own screen or module. Instead, it surfaces naturally at the moments where protection gaps become visible — through the mechanics the user is already engaging with. The goal is to make insurance feel like a logical next step in the user's progression, not a sales pitch.
How Insurance Appears Across the App
Inside the Stress Test (Onboarding) The stress test already shows how finances break under pressure. Insurance gaps are part of that story. When the simulation reveals that a medical emergency or car accident would wipe out the user's savings and derail their goals, the app flags the gap — not as a product recommendation, but as a risk.
"A single ER visit without health coverage could cost $2,700+. That eliminates your emergency fund and pushes your Move-Out Goal back by 5 months. Protection gap: High."
The user sees insurance the way they see credit or savings — as a risk category that affects their goals. This reframes insurance from "something responsible adults buy" to "the thing standing between me and losing everything I've built."
Inside the Conversational AI When a user asks goal-related questions, the AI factors in protection gaps naturally:
"Can I afford to move out in 6 months?" "Your savings are on track, but you currently have no renters' insurance. If something happens to your belongings after move-in, you'd face $3,000–$5,000 in replacement costs with no buffer. Renters' insurance averages about $15/month — adding it keeps your risk level stable without affecting your timeline."
"How much does life insurance actually cost?" "For someone your age and health profile, a $250,000 term policy typically runs $15–$25/month — significantly less than most people expect. Want me to show how it fits into your current budget?"
The AI debunks cost misconceptions in real time using the user's actual financial data. Research shows young adults overestimate life insurance costs by 10–12x — this is where that myth dies, in a conversation, not a classroom.
Inside Smart Alternatives (Big Purchases) When a user is considering a major purchase — a car, a move, a large commitment — the alternatives engine can include insurance as part of the smarter path:
User considers buying a $16,000 car. Alternative B: "Buy the $12,000 option and use the $80/month savings to cover full auto + renters' insurance. You're protected and your goal stays on track."
Insurance becomes part of the "better yes" — not an additional expense, but a reallocation that reduces overall risk.

Each unlock is a 30-second insight, not a course. The user sees what's relevant to their stage, why it matters for their goals, and what it actually costs. That's it.
Inside Daily Builds (Habits) Occasionally, a daily build can be insurance-related — lightweight and educational:
💡 Did you know? — "Renters' insurance covers your laptop, phone, and belongings if they're stolen — even outside your apartment. Average cost: $15/month." (15 sec read)
📋 Quick Check — "Do you know what your auto insurance deductible is? Tap to learn why it matters for your emergency fund sizing." (20 sec)
These aren't daily — they rotate in once or twice a month alongside other financial literacy builds. Enough to educate, not enough to annoy.
Why This Approach Works for the Hackathon
The app doesn't sell insurance. It reveals when the absence of insurance is the risk that threatens the user's goals — and then makes the real cost visible in a way that shatters misconceptions. Research shows only 1 in 4 Gen Z adults can define basic insurance terms like "deductible," and most overestimate costs dramatically. fortifi solves both problems not through education modules, but through the mechanics the user is already using every day.
For a sponsor who is an insurance company, this is the pitch: fortifi creates the moment of clarity that turns a young adult from someone who ignores insurance into someone who asks for it — because they saw exactly what happens to their goals without it.
